                            <p>The Senate Committee on Appropriations approved the $55.65 billion FY 2016 THUD appropriations bill, making the measure available for Senate floor consideration. A number of amendments were offered to increase funding for Amtrak, TIGER grants, New Starts, and Passenger Train Control implementation, but none were adopted.&nbsp; A partisan battle in the Senate over sequestration may prevent the THUD bill from getting Senate floor time.&nbsp; If that occurs, it is likely that USDOT programs, as well as many other federal agency programs, would be funded through a Continuing Resolution at last year&rsquo;s levels as of the <span data-term="goog_58716928">October 1</span> start of the new fiscal year.&nbsp;The full House previously approved its version of the bill on June 10.                             <p><strong>Senate Committee Approves Six-Year Transportation Bill</strong></p>
                            <p>The Senate Environment and Public Works Committee unanimously approved a six-year surface transportation reauthorization, called the DRIVE Act (S. 1647). The bill proposes to spend $278 billion on transportation projects over the six-year period, or about $46 billion a year, which is slightly above current MAP-21 spending levels. However, the bill doesn&rsquo;t contain a proposal to fill the looming Highway Trust Fund shortfall. Similar to a mechanism devised for the WRRDA bill last year, the Senate&rsquo;s bill contains the &ldquo;next best thing to earmarks,&rdquo; authority for FHWA to submit a list of &ldquo;projects of high importance&rdquo; to the House and Senate authorizing committees, which would then approve $350 million worth of those projects annually. For more: <a target="_blank" href="http://www.epw.senate.gov/public/index.cfm/press-releases-republican?ID=ED113C2A-EFA6-45B2-8466-FF6F62B2286F">DRIVE Act Press Release and Summary</a>, <a target="_blank" href="http://www.epw.senate.gov/public/index.cfm/press-releases-republican?ID=DE5E2075-0929-4E65-8E53-0351DA0CF3B8">Senators Inhofe and Boxer&rsquo;s Statement</a>, or <a target="_blank" href="http://www.transportation.gov/fastlane/sec-foxx-response-epw-drive-act">Secretary Anthony Foxx Statement</a>. At some point in July, Congress will have to pass another extension of MAP-21 since the latest short-term bill expires on <span data-term="goog_58716929">July 31</span>.&nbsp; One big difference with the next extension is that Congress will have to identify at least $10B in new revenue or spending cuts in order to fund the highway and transit programs through the end of the calendar year.&nbsp;</p>

                            <p><strong>U.S. Conference of Mayors 83<sup>rd</sup> Annual Meeting</strong></p>
                            <p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p>
                            <p><strong><em>Policies Adopted</em></strong></p>
                            <p>More than 100 resolutions were adopted by the Mayors, covering a wide range of topics including My Brother&rsquo;s Keeper, Ending Veterans&rsquo; Homelessness, Moving to Work, Transportation Reauthorization, Ending Sequestration, Climate Change, and Municipal Broadband, along with dozens of other subjects. For more: <a target="_blank" href="http://usmayors.org/resolutions/83rd_Conference/">USCM Adopted Resolutions.</a></p>
                            <p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p>
                            <p><strong><em>Speakers</em></strong></p>
                            <p><em>President Barak Obama</em>: On working with Mayors to achieve mutual policy goals, he said, &ldquo;<em>On America&rsquo;s most important economic priorities&hellip; cities are not standing still. You are moving forward. So I want to say two things to you. Number one &ndash; thank you&hellip; Second, I also want to say don&rsquo;t stop now, because we&rsquo;ve still got a lot more work to do</em>.&rdquo; The President spoke about the progress that has been made on a wide range of topics, including climate change, My Brother&rsquo;s Keeper, clean energy, the minimum wage, and high-tech manufacturing. He stressed the importance of promoting new infrastructure investments, boosting domestic programs, and ending homelessness. He ended by saying, &ldquo;I&rsquo;m a city guy,&rdquo; and that no one&rsquo;s zip code should determine their destiny. For more: <a target="_blank" href="https://www.whitehouse.gov/blog/2015/06/19/president-obama-addresses-us-conference-mayors">President&rsquo;s Remarks</a> or <a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTnBMusMbfs&amp;feature=youtu.be&amp;t=10585">President Obama Video</a>.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Hillary Clinton:</em> She spoke about her past work with Mayors, including her &ldquo;dear friend&rdquo; Tom Menino and &ldquo;our friend&rdquo; Joe Riley. She declared, &ldquo;If I am president, America&rsquo;s Mayors will always have a friend in the White House.&rdquo; She believes that we need to institute &ldquo;Flexible Federalism&rdquo; that empowers and connects communities, and that Mayors are a vital part of the &ldquo;Four Fights&rdquo; at the center of her campaign: (1) Building an economy for <span data-term="goog_58716933">tomorrow</span>, not yesterday, (2) Strengthening America&rsquo;s families, (3) Harness all of our power, our smarts, and our values to continue to lead the world, and (4) Revitalizing our democracy back here at home. For more: <a target="_blank" href="https://medium.com/@HillaryClinton/hillary-clinton-we-can-t-hide-from-hard-truths-on-race-96ce2257fe5a">Secretary Clinton&rsquo;s Remarks</a> or <a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XuMhk4isZFw&amp;feature=youtu.be&amp;t=765">Clinton Video</a>.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Mayor Rahm Emanuel:</em> Chicago Mayor Rahm Emanuel spoke to the Conference about the importance of education, saying that there are two economies: one for skilled labor, one for unskilled. While education at all levels should be promoted, he emphasized the need to invest in preschool and the post-High School years as particularly important. For more: <a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fI5ga0tYBEg&amp;feature=youtu.be&amp;t=2085">Mayor Emanuel Video</a>.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Mayor Ralph Becker:</em> Salt Lake City Mayor Ralph Becker, who serves as President of the National League of Cities, thanked the Conference for closely working with NLC on many issues, in line with the theme of his Presidency, &ldquo;Connecting.&rdquo; For more: <a target="_blank" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=whxEkbOX19A&amp;feature=youtu.be&amp;t=5200">Mayor Becker Video</a>.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><strong><em>Task Forces and Seminars</em></strong></p>
                            <p><em>Jobs, Education, and the Workforce Labor</em>: Secretary Thomas Perez spoke to the Committee about the &ldquo;skills super-highway&rdquo; needed to boost workforce development. He talked about the need to make community college more affordable, the need to focus on &ldquo;career pathways&rdquo; more than individual jobs, and the benefits of Registered Apprenticeships. Tacoma Mayor Marilyn Strickland talked about the link between transportation and job opportunities.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Children, Health, and Human Services</em>: Michael Botticelli, Director of the White House Office of National Drug Control Policy, spoke to the Committee about the national opioid epidemic and outlined federal resources available to help Mayors combat this pressing issue on-the-ground, especially in the Northeast and Midwest, where the epidemic is most pronounced.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Hunger and Homelessness Task Force</em>: The Task Force: Chaired by Santa Barbara Mayor Helene Schneider, focused on work being done in her city and others, like Albuquerque and Anaheim, to reduce homelessness. Amy Sawyer with the U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness, talked about the progress made, and work left to be done, since the Council&rsquo;s comprehensive &ldquo;Opening Doors&rdquo; strategy was released in June 2010.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Mayors and Police Chiefs Task Force:</em> White House Office of Intergovernmental Affairs Director Jerry Abramson joined Fresno Mayor Ashley Swearengin and Gary, IN Mayor Karen Freeman-Wilson to discuss the findings of the Report of the President&rsquo;s Task Force on 21st Century Policing and the importance of improving relationships between law enforcement and their communities.</p>
                            <p><em>&nbsp;</em></p>
                            <p><em>Veterans Affairs Task Force:</em> Patrick O&rsquo;Brien, Director of the Defense Department&rsquo;s Office of Economic Adjustment, advised Task Force members to contact his office if they hear of a pending base closure, or contract or workforce reduction in their community. His office continues to follow BRAC discussions in Congress closely.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Technology and Innovation Task Force</em>: Megan Smith, U.S. Chief Technology Officer, highlighted the work that cities are engaging in around the country to promote tech literacy, including Code Louisville. She called on cities to lift up IT employees that might be &ldquo;buried&rdquo; in the lower levels of government, and include them in the conversation at the executive levels. She expressed concern about the increasing &ldquo;vendorizing&rdquo; of tech at all levels of government &ndash; local, state, and federal.</p>
                            <p>&nbsp;</p>
                            <p><em>Ports and Exports Task Force</em>: Steven Stockton, Director of Civil Works for the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, spoke to the Task Force about the importance that inland waterways and barges play in promoting U.S. exports, especially since the enactment of WRRDA.</p>
